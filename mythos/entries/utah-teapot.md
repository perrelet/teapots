Modelled in 1975 by Martin Newell from an ordinary Melitta teapot on his kitchen
table (his wife Sandra suggested it), the Utah teapot became computer graphics'
default object — simple enough to render, complex enough to show off shading and
curvature. It now hides as an Easter egg across the field: in *Toy Story*, in *The
Simpsons* "Homer³", in countless 3-D packages as a built-in primitive. The
`ontology-shift` (material → virtual) is the point: a real pot became the thing
rendered *before anything else exists* — a near-creation-myth for computing, and a
keystone of the `reference-object` mode.
