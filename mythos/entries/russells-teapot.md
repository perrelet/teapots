Russell's analogy: a china teapot too small for any telescope, orbiting the Sun
between Earth and Mars. Because its existence cannot be *disproven*, someone might
insist on it — yet the burden of proof lies with the one making the claim, not
with the skeptic asked to accept it.

He wrote it in 1952 for *Illustrated* magazine, which commissioned the essay *Is
There a God?*, paid for it, and then never ran it; it stayed unpublished until
1997, so its fame is almost entirely an internet-age afterlife. The argument turns
on three sentences, and it is worth resisting the usual truncation to the first —
the force is in what follows:

> "If I were to suggest that between the Earth and Mars there is a china teapot
> revolving about the sun in an elliptical orbit, nobody would be able to disprove
> my assertion provided I were careful to add that the teapot is too small to be
> revealed even by our most powerful telescopes. But if I were to go on to say
> that, since my assertion cannot be disproved, it is intolerable presumption on
> the part of human reason to doubt it, I should rightly be thought to be talking
> nonsense. If, however, the existence of such a teapot were affirmed in ancient
> books, taught as the sacred truth every Sunday… hesitation to believe in its
> existence would become a mark of eccentricity."

The undetectability is the whole point: a claim engineered to be unfalsifiable,
and therefore one no one is *obliged* to believe. Dawkins made it a centrepiece of
*The God Delusion* (2006); it is the twin of Hitchens's razor, and the direct
ancestor of the Invisible Pink Unicorn and the Flying Spaghetti Monster — the same
argument with a funnier object.

## In the braid

This is the root the whole collection grows from: the one entry with no outgoing
edges, because everything else points back at it. [[perrelets-teapot]] **responds
to** it and turns the burden of proof inside out; Gong's *Flying Teapot* and
*Operation Cosmic Teapot* **allude to** it. It fixes the teapot as the canonical
object of *unfalsifiable belief* — and, in a happy accident of the sky, finds a
literal counterpart in the [[sagittarius-teapot]], a real teapot of stars steaming
into the galaxy while Russell's hypothetical one hides just out of view.
